## In Indian Constitutional Law

Under Article 32 of the Indian Constitution, the Supreme Court has the power to issue writs including habeas corpus for enforcement of fundamental rights. Under Article 226, High Courts have similar powers with a wider scope. The writ of habeas corpus is considered a bulwark against illegal detention and is one of the most important tools for protecting personal liberty.

## Historical Significance in India

The Emergency period of 1975-77 saw the most dramatic curtailment of habeas corpus rights in independent India. In the infamous ADM Jabalpur case (1976), the Supreme Court, by a 4:1 majority, held that during Emergency, the right to approach the court through habeas corpus could be suspended. This decision was later described by the court itself as "seriously erroneous" in the 2017 Puttaswamy (Privacy) judgment. The lone dissent by Justice H.R. Khanna became legendary and is considered one of the finest moments of judicial courage in Indian legal history.

## Grounds for Habeas Corpus

A habeas corpus petition can be filed on various grounds including: detention without adequate legal authority, detention in violation of constitutional safeguards, detention by a private person without authority of law, and failure to produce before a magistrate within the statutory time period.
