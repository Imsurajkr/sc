## Constitutional Basis

The suo motu power of Indian constitutional courts flows primarily from Articles 32 and 226 of the Constitution and has been read expansively by the courts themselves. The power allows courts to transcend the adversarial model of litigation and act in the public interest when they become aware of a constitutional violation through media reports, letters from individuals, or other sources.

## Controversy Around Suo Motu Powers

The exercise of suo motu powers has attracted both praise and criticism. Proponents argue that it allows courts to protect rights even when affected individuals lack the resources or knowledge to approach the court. Critics argue that it blurs the line between the judicial and executive functions, allowing courts to take on policy questions beyond their institutional competence. The extent to which suo motu actions can be effectively monitored and followed up is also a recurring concern.

## Notable Suo Motu Actions

Among the most significant suo motu actions of the Supreme Court have been: taking cognisance of the Bhopal gas tragedy; the right to food case during the 2002 drought; the Pegasus spyware case; and various matters relating to COVID-19 relief during the pandemic.
