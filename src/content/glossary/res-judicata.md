term: "Res Judicata"

## Elements of Res Judicata Under the CPC

Section 11 of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 codifies the doctrine of res judicata in India. For the doctrine to apply, the following conditions must be satisfied:
- The matter must have been directly and substantially in issue in the former suit.
- The former suit must have been between the same parties or parties claiming under them.
- The parties must have litigated under the same title.
- The court that decided the former suit must have been competent to try the subsequent suit.
- The matter must have been heard and finally decided.

## Constructive Res Judicata

An important extension of the doctrine is "constructive res judicata" — a point that might and ought to have been raised in the earlier proceedings, but was not, is deemed to have been decided against the party who did not raise it. This prevents piecemeal litigation where a party deliberately holds back arguments to reopen a case later.

## Res Judicata and Constitutional Courts

The doctrine applies in modified form to writ petitions before constitutional courts. While a habeas corpus petition can generally be filed repeatedly since liberty is at stake, other writ petitions are subject to res judicata principles. The Supreme Court has held that a second writ petition on the same cause of action, between the same parties, will generally be barred by constructive res judicata.
