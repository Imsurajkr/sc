## The Question of ISP Liability

The Supreme Court's recent engagement with the question of internet service provider liability marks a turning point in India's evolving digital jurisprudence. The court's order, delivered in a batch of petitions concerning website blocking directives under the Information Technology Act, 2000, has significant implications for how ISPs navigate their dual obligations — to comply with government orders on one hand and to protect the rights of their users on the other.

The petitioners in the case argued that ISPs are being made unwitting instruments of censorship, required to block entire websites or platforms based on administrative orders without any judicial oversight. This, they contended, violates the principles laid down by the Supreme Court in Shreya Singhal v. Union of India (2015), where the court struck down Section 66A of the IT Act and read down the blocking provisions of Section 69A to require procedural safeguards.

## Proportionality and Collateral Damage

A central theme in the court's analysis was the principle of proportionality. When an ISP blocks a website URL to comply with a government order, it may inadvertently block thousands of other URLs hosted on the same IP address or domain. This "over-blocking" or collateral damage to free speech is a well-documented phenomenon that courts around the world have struggled to address.

The court observed that the blocking rules under Section 69A must be read in light of the constitutional guarantee of free speech under Article 19(1)(a). Any restriction, including blocking of content, must satisfy the tests of legality, necessity, and proportionality. A blanket block that affects unrelated content cannot be justified merely because some content on a platform is deemed objectionable.

## Implications for Digital Rights

This judgment has significant implications beyond ISP liability. It signals that the Supreme Court is willing to apply rigorous constitutional scrutiny to government actions in the digital sphere. It reinforces the principle that the internet cannot be governed through the same broad discretionary powers that might be permissible in other contexts. The challenge now lies in translating these principles into consistent practice across the hundreds of blocking orders issued every year.
