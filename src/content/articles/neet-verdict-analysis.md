## The Immediate Aftermath

The Supreme Court's August 2024 order declining a wholesale NEET re-test was met with mixed reactions. Students who had not been implicated in any irregularity were relieved; those in affected centres felt let down. The National Testing Agency, however, faced sustained institutional pressure. Within weeks of the judgment, the government replaced the NTA director general and announced a comprehensive review of the agency's operations.

## The Radhakrishnan Committee Recommendations

The high-level committee chaired by former ISRO chairman K. Radhakrishnan submitted its report in September 2024. Its key recommendations included: decentralisation of examination centres, end-to-end digital encryption of question papers, biometric verification of candidates, and a complete overhaul of the NTA's governance structure. The government accepted most recommendations, though the timeline for implementation was criticised as too slow.

## Structural Changes to the NTA

The NTA's restructuring has involved separating its examination and administrative functions, creating an independent integrity unit, and establishing real-time monitoring of all examination processes. The agency has also piloted computer-based testing for a larger proportion of its examinations, reducing the vulnerability to physical paper leaks. Whether these changes will be sufficient to prevent future leaks remains to be seen.

## What Has Not Changed

Despite the systemic changes, the fundamental problem — high-stakes examinations determining access to scarce seats in elite institutions — remains intact. The enormous financial rewards for successful cheating continue to incentivise corruption. The coaching industry, which turns examination preparation into an all-consuming commercial enterprise, continues to flourish. Students from disadvantaged backgrounds continue to face structural barriers that go far beyond examination integrity. The NEET verdict may have addressed some symptoms but has not touched the underlying pathologies of India's examination culture.
