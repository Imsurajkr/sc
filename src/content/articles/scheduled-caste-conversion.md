## The Presidential Orders and their Religious Restriction

At the heart of the case lies the Constitution (Scheduled Castes) Order, 1950, as amended over the years, which determines who qualifies as a Scheduled Caste for the purposes of reservations in education, employment, and political representation. The Order originally restricted SC status to Hindus, was later expanded to include Sikhs (1956) and Buddhists (1990), but continues to exclude Christians and Muslims.

The exclusion rests on the assumption that caste discrimination is intrinsic to Hinduism (and by extension, some dharmic religions) but does not exist, or exists in a different form, within egalitarian Abrahamic faiths. Petitioners challenging this exclusion argue that caste is a social reality that persists regardless of religion — a Dalit Christian or Dalit Muslim continues to face the same social and economic discrimination as a Dalit Hindu.

## The Evidence on Caste Discrimination After Conversion

The National Commission for Religious and Linguistic Minorities (the Ranganath Misra Commission) and the Justice Rajinder Sachar Committee both documented the persistence of caste discrimination among converted communities. Dalit Christians and Dalit Muslims, despite having converted generations ago, continue to face social ostracism, economic exclusion, and untouchability in practice. These findings form a critical part of the petitioners' case.

The government's opposing argument draws a distinction between social discrimination and legal disability. It argues that Scheduled Caste status was designed to address the specific form of discrimination rooted in the Hindu caste system's ritual hierarchy, and that extending it to converts from other religions would dilute its remedial purpose.

## What the Court Decides Will Affect Millions

The Constitution Bench's ruling will have immediate and practical consequences for an estimated 20-30 million Dalit Christians and Dalit Muslims in India. A ruling in favour of the petitioners could make them eligible for reservations and other affirmative action benefits that have been denied to their communities for over seven decades. A ruling against could entrench a distinction that has been criticised as both legally tenuous and socially harmful. The stakes could not be higher.
