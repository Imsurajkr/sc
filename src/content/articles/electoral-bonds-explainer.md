## What Were Electoral Bonds?

Electoral bonds were bearer instruments introduced by the Finance Act, 2017 and operationalised through the Electoral Bond Scheme, 2018. They were available in denominations from Rs 1,000 to Rs 1 crore, could be purchased by any citizen or company, and could be donated to any registered political party. The scheme's key feature was anonymity — while the State Bank of India knew who had purchased bonds, the Election Commission and the public did not.

## The Numbers

Between March 2018 and February 2024, electoral bonds worth approximately Rs 16,518 crore were purchased. A disproportionate share — nearly 55% — went to the ruling Bharatiya Janata Party. The opposition Indian National Congress received approximately 10%. Data released pursuant to the Supreme Court's order revealed that several large corporate donors had received significant government contracts, regulatory approvals, or policy benefits around the time of their donations.

## Why the Court Struck it Down

The Constitution Bench unanimously found the scheme unconstitutional on two main grounds:

**Right to Information**: The court held that voters have a fundamental right under Article 19(1)(a) to know who funds political parties. This information is essential for voters to make an informed choice at the ballot box. Anonymity in political funding is thus not a mere procedural matter but one that goes to the heart of democratic accountability.

**Proportionality**: The court applied the proportionality test from its privacy jurisprudence and found that even if protecting donor privacy had some legitimacy, making donations completely anonymous was not a proportionate means of achieving that end. Less restrictive alternatives existed, such as requiring disclosure above a threshold amount.

## Aftermath and Continuing Questions

The Election Commission's disclosure of bond data triggered significant political controversy and investigative journalism. The data revealed patterns suggesting a possible quid pro quo between corporate donations and regulatory or policy outcomes, though no court has yet definitively established such a connection. The question of how to fund political parties in a transparent manner without exposing donors to victimisation remains unresolved.
