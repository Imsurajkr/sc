## The Numbers at a Glance

As of January 2025, the Supreme Court of India has 33 judges against a sanctioned strength of 34. While near full strength at the apex court, the situation at the high courts tells a different story: across India's 25 high courts, the combined working strength is approximately 770 judges against a sanctioned strength of 1,122 — a vacancy rate of nearly 31%.

This means that more than one in three judicial posts in the high courts is unfilled, creating cascading effects on the pace of justice delivery. Cases take longer to be decided, bail applications take longer to be heard, and urgent matters are routinely delayed for want of judicial time.

## Trends in Appointments

Data from the Supreme Court Collegium's recommendations over the past five years reveals several patterns. First, the gap between recommendation and appointment has widened — what used to take weeks now routinely takes months or years. Second, the government's rate of returning collegium recommendations has increased, even after the 99th Constitutional Amendment and NJAC Act were struck down in 2015. Third, several high courts — particularly those in smaller states — consistently operate at below 60% of their sanctioned strength.

## The Collegium System Under Scrutiny

The collegium system, which gives the Chief Justice and senior judges of the Supreme Court the primary say in judicial appointments, has faced criticism from multiple directions. Critics on the right argue it creates a self-perpetuating oligarchy insulated from democratic accountability. Critics on the left argue that the government's influence over the collegium's choices distorts the pool of candidates. Data shows that the diversity of the judiciary — in terms of gender, region, and social background — remains limited, though there has been modest improvement in recent years.

## What the Data Does Not Capture

Behind the statistics are individual stories: the first-generation litigant in Bihar who has been waiting eight years for her property dispute to be resolved; the undertrial prisoner in Maharashtra who has been in jail for three years awaiting trial on charges that carry a maximum sentence of two years; the small business owner in Tamil Nadu whose insolvency petition has been pending for four years while his business deteriorates. Judicial vacancies are not an administrative problem — they are a human rights crisis, playing out one delayed hearing at a time.
