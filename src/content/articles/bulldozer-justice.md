## The Phenomenon of Bulldozer Justice

The term "bulldozer justice" entered India's political vocabulary around 2020, when the Uttar Pradesh government began demolishing the homes and businesses of individuals accused of serious crimes, particularly those related to communal violence. The governments of Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, and other BJP-ruled states followed suit. Proponents argued it was a show of strength against criminals; opponents argued it was extrajudicial punishment targeted primarily at the Muslim community.

The Supreme Court's engagement with this practice was initially cautious. The court repeatedly stayed specific demolitions but hesitated to issue broader guidelines. In November 2024, a two-judge bench of Justices B.R. Gavai and K.V. Viswanathan finally issued comprehensive guidelines after hearing the matter over several hearings.

## The Constitutional Framework

The court's guidelines rest on several constitutional foundations. First, Article 21 protects the right to shelter as a component of the right to life. Second, the principles of natural justice — that no one can be condemned without a hearing — apply to demolitions just as they do to any adverse state action. Third, the doctrine of proportionality requires that even legitimate regulatory action (like demolishing an illegal structure) must be proportionate and must not be used as a pretext for punitive action.

## What the Guidelines Say

The court's guidelines are detailed and operational. They require prior notice of at least 15 days, an opportunity to be heard, a written order with reasons, and adequate time to approach higher courts for relief. Critically, the guidelines prohibit any linkage between a demolition order and a criminal accusation — a demolition must be based solely on a violation of building regulations, not on the fact that the occupant is an accused.

## The Implementation Challenge

The most significant challenge is ensuring that guidelines issued by the Supreme Court translate into actual change on the ground. State governments have historically been reluctant to abandon the political utility of demolitions, which generate images of decisive action against criminals. The court has indicated it will monitor compliance through contempt proceedings, but the practical effectiveness of this mechanism remains to be tested.
