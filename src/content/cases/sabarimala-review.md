## Background

In September 2018, a five-judge Constitutional Bench of the Supreme Court, by a 4:1 majority, held that the exclusion of women between the ages of 10 and 50 from the Sabarimala temple in Kerala was unconstitutional. The majority held that the practice violated Articles 14, 15, 17, and 25 of the Constitution.

## Review Petitions

Following the judgment, thousands of review petitions were filed. In November 2019, a five-judge bench, by a 3:2 majority, referred the matter to a larger nine-judge bench to settle overarching constitutional questions including:

1. What is the interplay between freedom of religion under Articles 25 and 26 and other fundamental rights?
2. Whether the right to equality and non-discrimination under Articles 14 and 15 override religious denominational rights?
3. Whether the "essential religious practices" doctrine needs to be revisited?

## Significance

The nine-judge bench's ruling will have far-reaching implications not just for Sabarimala but also for cases involving entry of Muslim women into mosques and Parsi women who marry outside the faith into fire temples. The court is essentially being asked to determine the limits of judicial review of religious customs.

## Current Status

Arguments before the nine-judge bench have been heard in stages. The bench is yet to deliver its judgment. Multiple amici curiae have been appointed to assist the court.
