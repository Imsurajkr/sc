## Background

In July 2021, an international consortium of journalists published the Pegasus Project — a report revealing that NSO Group's Pegasus spyware had been used to surveil journalists, human rights activists, politicians, and lawyers across the world. The report indicated that India was among the countries using the spyware, with targets including political opponents and journalists.

## Court's Intervention

Multiple petitions were filed in the Supreme Court seeking an independent investigation. Chief Justice N.V. Ramana, speaking for the bench, observed that "the right to privacy is a fundamental right under Article 21" and that "if the allegations are true, it is a serious matter."

## Technical Committee

The court constituted a three-member technical expert committee, chaired by former Supreme Court judge R.V. Raveendran, to:
- Examine whether Pegasus spyware was used on Indian devices.
- Identify those whose phones were hacked.
- Recommend steps to prevent illegal surveillance.

## Outcome

The technical committee examined 29 phones submitted by petitioners and found malware on some devices but could not conclusively establish it was Pegasus. The Union government refused to cooperate with the committee citing national security. The committee's full findings were submitted to the court under seal in August 2022 and the case was closed.

## Legacy

The case established important principles: that national security cannot be a blanket justification for surveillance, and that any surveillance must comply with the requirements of Article 21. These principles continue to guide surveillance-related litigation.
