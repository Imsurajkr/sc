## Background

Beginning around 2020, several state governments — particularly Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, and Rajasthan — began demolishing properties of individuals accused of criminal offences or communal violence. These demolitions, dubbed "bulldozer justice" by the media, were presented by governments as enforcement of building regulations, but critics argued they were extrajudicial punishments targeting specific communities.

## Supreme Court's Intervention

The Supreme Court took suo motu cognisance of the matter and also admitted petitions challenging the demolitions. The court expressed alarm at the "alarming trend" of executive action bypassing judicial processes.

## Guidelines Issued

In its November 2024 judgment, the court issued comprehensive guidelines:

1. **Prior notice**: A minimum of 15 days' notice must be given before any demolition.
2. **Hearing**: The affected person must be given an opportunity to be heard.
3. **Response time**: The person must be given time to challenge the demolition order.
4. **Written orders**: Demolition orders must be in writing with reasons.
5. **No collective punishment**: The property of an entire family cannot be demolished for the alleged crime of one member.
6. **Independent of criminal process**: Demolitions cannot be linked to criminal accusations; they must be based solely on violations of building regulations.

## Significance

The judgment is a landmark ruling on the right to housing and due process. The court emphasised that in a constitutional democracy, the state cannot act as judge, jury, and executioner. The guidelines set important guardrails against arbitrary executive action.
