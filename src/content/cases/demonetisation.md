## Background

On November 8, 2016, Prime Minister Narendra Modi announced that Rs 500 and Rs 1000 currency notes would cease to be legal tender from midnight that day. This demonetisation exercise, which invalidated approximately 86% of India's currency in circulation, was challenged through over 50 petitions in the Supreme Court.

## Legal Issues

The petitioners raised several constitutional and statutory challenges:

1. Whether the government had the power to demonetise currency notes under Section 26(2) of the Reserve Bank of India Act, 1934.
2. Whether the decision was taken in proper consultation with the RBI.
3. Whether the notification was arbitrary and violated Articles 14, 19, and 21.
4. Whether the short window for exchange of old currency was proportionate.

## The Judgment

The four-judge majority upheld the demonetisation, finding that:
- The government had the power to demonetise under the RBI Act.
- The decision was taken after proper consultation with the RBI Board.
- The measures were proportionate to the legitimate aims of curbing black money.

## Justice Nagarathna's Dissent

In a significant dissent, Justice B.V. Nagarathna held that demonetisation of such a large proportion of currency could only have been done by Parliament through legislation, not by the executive through a notification. She found the process legally flawed, though she acknowledged the laudable aims of the exercise.

## Legacy

The 6-year delay between the demonetisation and the judgment was widely criticised. The dissent by Justice Nagarathna is considered by legal scholars to be the more legally sound position, raising important questions about parliamentary oversight of major economic decisions.
