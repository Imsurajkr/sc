## Background

In February 2022, Muslim girl students in Karnataka were barred from attending pre-university colleges wearing hijab after the college development committees issued orders prescribing a uniform dress code. The Karnataka High Court upheld the ban in March 2022, holding that wearing the hijab was not an essential religious practice in Islam. The students appealed to the Supreme Court.

## The Split Verdict

The two-judge bench delivered diametrically opposite opinions:

**Justice Hemant Gupta** upheld the High Court's decision and found:
- Wearing hijab is not an essential religious practice.
- Educational institutions can impose reasonable dress codes.
- The government order was a reasonable restriction on the fundamental right to religion.

**Justice Sudhanshu Dhulia** struck down the ban and found:
- The issue should not have been examined through the lens of "essential religious practice."
- A student's right to wear hijab is part of her right to privacy, dignity, and education.
- The ban was an unnecessary intrusion that forced girls to choose between their faith and education.

## Referral to Larger Bench

Due to the split verdict, the matter was referred to a larger bench. As of the date of this report, the case has not been listed before a larger bench and the Karnataka government's order remains in force.

## Significance

The case raises profound questions about the scope of the "essential religious practice" doctrine and whether courts should be determining what constitutes a mandatory practice within a religion. Justice Dhulia's opinion is widely regarded by legal scholars as a more constitutionally sound approach rooted in individual autonomy.
