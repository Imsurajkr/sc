## Background

The Citizenship Amendment Act, 2019 (CAA) was passed by Parliament on December 11, 2019 and received Presidential assent on December 12, 2019. It amends the Citizenship Act, 1955 to provide an expedited pathway to Indian citizenship for members of Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain, Parsi, and Christian communities who fled religious persecution from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan before December 31, 2014.

## Constitutional Challenge

The petitioners, including the Indian Union Muslim League, the Kerala government, and various civil society organisations, challenge the Act on multiple grounds:

- **Article 14**: The Act creates an arbitrary classification based on religion that violates the right to equality.
- **Secular character**: The exclusion of Muslims from the Act strikes at the secular character of the Constitution.
- **Article 21**: The Act, combined with the proposed National Register of Citizens, could render Muslims stateless.

## Government's Position

The Union of India argues that the classification is based on a reasonable nexus to the object of the legislation — providing refuge to persecuted minorities who cannot seek protection from the governments of their own countries. The government contends that since the three named countries are Islamic republics, Muslims cannot be considered persecuted minorities.

## Current Status

The matter has been admitted and is pending before a five-judge Constitution Bench. Arguments have commenced but a final hearing date is yet to be fixed.
