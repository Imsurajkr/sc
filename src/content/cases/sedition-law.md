## Background

Section 124A of the Indian Penal Code, which makes sedition a criminal offence punishable with up to life imprisonment, was enacted by the British colonial government in 1870 primarily to suppress the Indian independence movement. It has been used extensively against journalists, activists, and government critics.

## Previous Supreme Court Orders

In May 2022, a three-judge bench headed by Chief Justice N.V. Ramana stayed all proceedings under Section 124A and directed the government to reconsider the law. The government had initially indicated willingness to review the provision, but subsequent administrations have been less forthcoming.

## New Criminal Laws

The Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 (which replaced the IPC from July 1, 2024) does not contain a provision specifically labelled "sedition" but includes Section 152, which criminalises acts endangering the "sovereignty, unity and integrity of India." Critics argue that Section 152 is essentially the old sedition law with new language.

## Current Legal Position

The constitutional challenge now encompasses both the old Section 124A and the new Section 152. The petitioners argue that any law criminalising criticism of the government without requiring incitement to violence violates Article 19(1)(a) (freedom of speech). The government argues that such laws are necessary for national security.

## Significance

A ruling on this case will significantly impact the legal landscape for freedom of speech in India and determine the extent to which criticism of government policies can be criminalised.
