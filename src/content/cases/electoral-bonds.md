## Background

The Electoral Bonds Scheme was introduced in 2018, allowing individuals and corporations to purchase bonds from the State Bank of India and donate them to political parties. The scheme promised anonymity to donors, with the SBI being the only entity that could match bonds to donors.

## The Judgment

On February 15, 2024, a unanimous five-judge Constitution Bench struck down the Electoral Bonds Scheme. Chief Justice D.Y. Chandrachud, writing for the court, held:

- **Right to Information**: Voters have a constitutionally protected right under Article 19(1)(a) to know who funds political parties, as this information is essential for making an informed electoral choice.
- **Disproportionate restriction**: The scheme's anonymity provisions were not proportionate to the stated aim of curbing black money in elections.
- **Unlimited corporate donations**: The removal of the earlier cap on corporate donations violated the principle of free and fair elections.

## Post-Judgment Developments

Following the judgment, the Election Commission published data on electoral bond purchases and encashments as directed. The data revealed significant correlations between large donations and subsequent government contracts or policy decisions, sparking a nationwide political controversy.

## Significance

The Electoral Bonds judgment is considered one of the most significant constitutional rulings in recent years. It reaffirmed the right to information as a cornerstone of democratic governance and set important limits on the role of money in electoral politics.
