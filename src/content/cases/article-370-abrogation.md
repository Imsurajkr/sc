## Background

On August 5, 2019, the Union government issued a Presidential Order under Article 370, effectively abrogating the provision and stripping Jammu and Kashmir of its special status. The state was simultaneously bifurcated into two Union Territories — Jammu and Kashmir (with a legislature) and Ladakh (without a legislature). Multiple petitions were filed challenging these decisions.

## Constitutional Questions

The bench was tasked with determining:

1. Whether Article 370 was a temporary or permanent provision of the Constitution.
2. Whether the President had the power to abrogate Article 370 unilaterally.
3. Whether the bifurcation of a state without the consent of its legislature was constitutionally permissible.
4. Whether the imposition of President's Rule was a condition precedent for the abrogation.

## The Judgment

The five-judge bench, in a unanimous verdict, upheld the abrogation. Chief Justice D.Y. Chandrachud held that Article 370 was indeed a temporary provision that could be abrogated by the President. The court also upheld the bifurcation while directing the restoration of statehood to Jammu and Kashmir and the holding of elections.

## Significance

The judgment settled a decade-long constitutional question about the status of Article 370 and the sovereignty of the Indian Union over Jammu and Kashmir. Critics, however, argued that the court's reasoning bypassed the federal character of the Constitution by allowing the Centre to convert a state into Union Territories.
