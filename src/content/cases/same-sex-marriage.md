## Background

Following the Supreme Court's 2018 judgment decriminalising homosexuality (Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India), LGBTQ+ couples and activists filed petitions seeking recognition of same-sex marriages. The petitions primarily sought amendments to the Special Marriage Act, 1954 to include same-sex couples.

## The Hearings

The ten-day hearing before the Constitution Bench attracted significant public interest. The bench heard arguments about whether the Constitution recognises a fundamental right to marry, whether non-heterosexual couples have equal rights under Articles 14 and 21, and whether the court could read gender-neutral provisions into the Special Marriage Act.

## The Judgment

The five-judge bench delivered a split verdict. Chief Justice Chandrachud and Justice Kaul (in a minority) held that LGBTQ+ couples have a right to form unions but not necessarily to marry. The majority (Justices Bhat, Kohli, and Narasimha) held that no such right exists and that the matter must be left to Parliament.

Key findings:
- There is no fundamental right to marry under the Indian Constitution.
- The court cannot rewrite legislation; Parliament must amend the law.
- Queer couples have certain rights including the right to adopt, though this too was a contested point.

## Criticism and Reception

The judgment was criticised by LGBTQ+ advocates as a missed opportunity, while others praised the court's restraint in not usurping the legislative function. Chief Justice Chandrachud's comments during the hearing about the "holistic package of rights" for queer couples were seen as promising but not fulfilled by the final order.
